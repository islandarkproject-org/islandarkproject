About Island Ark Project

The Island Ark Project is a digital platform for preserving and continuing intangible cultural practices as an increasing number of island communities are forced to migrate because of climate change-­related destruction. The initiative is based on research done by its founders while interning at Harvard University's Berkman Center for Internet & Society in the summer of 2015.
"Our mission is to support climate change migrants in curating their culture and continuing traditions and community."


The Challenge

Climate change is threatening the world's island nations. From salt water intrusion into fresh water sources to increasingly powerful bad-weather events such as storms and droughts, these challenges will play an increasingly destructive role for many islands around the world. When islanders migrate in search of better living conditions, they risk losing thousands of years worth of lived cultural practices. This includes "intangible cultural heritage" such as dances, songs, recipes, oral histories, traditional craft knowledge, rituals, and so forth, as well as the cultural context and meaning behind all of these practices.

The Solution

In response to these problems, the Island Ark Project team is in the process of developing an online platform for cultural heritage protection. This web platform will function as a repository where cultural practices collected by local islanders can be safeguarded for current islanders and future generations. The digital nature of the platform will enable communities to continue to access information about immaterial heritage even if individual members lose their physical connection to the community.

Furthermore, the network of cultural entries on the web platform will be more than a simple documentation effort. Instead, families and communities will be able to discuss and contextualize their cultural practices on a secure and accessible site, which will keep culture “alive” as users explore and evaluate shared cultural practices together. This interaction will be guaranteed by a sophisticated and user­friendly system of permissions designed to protect the intellectual property of users; moreover, a safeguarding mechanism will be built into the platform so that only authorized users will be able to access “secret” or private community practices. Naturally, the final platform will be able to host many different types of media, including pictures, videos, text, and audio files.

We are especially interested in recognizing and encouraging the diversity of intangible heritage. Specific traditional rituals may differ between families, municipalities, and islands, and there is not one “correct” way of conducting a ritual or preparing a meal. Our innovative platform will allow for the kind of bottom-­up, democratized knowledge creation and curation that is necessary to replicate the multiplicity of immaterial cultural practices in a country.

Another important aspect of online cultural preservation centers on harnessing the skills of the younger generation. Our project will support inter-generational collaboration between technically­skilled youth and older generations in order to make traditional knowledge about intangible cultural practices available for all members of the community. Furthermore, since education and technical literacy are vital to the success of the project, we are developing programs to improve technical skills in rural or less-­connected areas. These trainings and workshops on basic computer and Internet skills will be provided to island communities starting in early 2016.


Partnerships

In September 2015 the Island Ark Project was fortunate to partner with the UNESCO Delegation to Palau, and in November 2015 the team presented about the project to many SIDS delegations and national commissions during the 38th session of the UNESCO General Conference. The Island Ark Project team will be traveling to Palau in March 2016 in order to test the beta version of the web platform and to observe the reactions of individuals and communities. Additional case studies will be conducted in 3 to 4 SIDS during the second half of 2016, with the goal of testing the success of our platform in a variety of islands with different developmental and technical limitations.
